A good system is invisible. You use it every day and never think about how it works. The moment you notice it, something has gone wrong — a seam has opened, an assumption has been violated, a piece has stopped fitting.

This is why the best systems feel less like inventions and more like discoveries. Someone found the shape that was already there and made it explicit. The rules were always latent in the material; the system just names them.

## Building versus finding

There is a useful fiction in making that separates building from finding. Builders start with an empty space and fill it. Finders start with a full space and clear it. In practice neither is purely one or the other, but the fiction helps you understand which mode you are in at any given moment.

When you are building, you are making decisions. Every decision forecloses other decisions. You are narrowing. When you are finding, you are observing. You are watching what the system wants to do when you stop fighting it.

> The system that endures is the one that reveals rather than imposes. It does not tell the material what to be. It asks the material what it already is.

The best design systems I have encountered feel found rather than built. The decisions they encode are not arbitrary — they come from close attention to how the components actually wanted to relate to each other.

## The cost of premature closure

Every system has a moment of closure — the point at which the rules are fixed and the space for revision collapses. Premature closure is one of the most common failure modes in design. You lock down the system before you understand it, and then you spend years working around the locked parts.

The antidote is to stay in the finding phase longer than is comfortable. Hold the rules loosely. Make the minimum number of decisions required to move forward. Leave the load-bearing decisions until last, when you have the most information.

This is hard. There is enormous social and institutional pressure to resolve ambiguity early. Stakeholders want specifications. Developers want interfaces. Managers want commitments. The person who says "I don't know yet" is seen as less competent than the person who says "here is the plan."

But the plan made with insufficient information is not a plan. It is a guess that has been given the social weight of a plan. And guesses made early, in the dark, with authority, are the most expensive kind.

## What patience actually means

Patience in system design is not passivity. It is active waiting — continuing to gather information, continuing to test assumptions, continuing to hold conclusions lightly — while also moving forward. You do not stop making decisions. You stop making irreversible ones.

The reversible decision is the patience decision. Can this be changed later without too much cost? Then make it now. Does this close off important options? Then wait.
